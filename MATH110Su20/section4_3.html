Section 4.3 Exponential Functions #3

Worksheet 4.3.1 Worksheet

1.
Find the equation of the exponential function passing through the points \((1,6)\) and \((4,24)\text{.}\) Solution

As the function is of the form \(y=a\cdot b^x\text{,}\) we see that

\begin{equation*} 24=ab^4 \text{ and } 6=ab^1 \end{equation*}

Dividing the two equations yields

\begin{equation*} \frac{24=ab^4}{6=ab^1} \quad \Rightarrow \quad 4 = b^3 \quad \Rightarrow \quad b = \sqrt[3]{4} \end{equation*}

Now that we know \(b\text{,}\) we can find \(a\) by using one of the above equations

\begin{equation*} 6=a\sqrt[3]{4}^1 \Rightarrow a = \frac{6}{\sqrt[3]{4}} \end{equation*}
\begin{equation*} y=\frac{6}{\sqrt[3]{4}}(\sqrt[3]{4})^x \end{equation*}

(It would be a good idea to check by graphing).

2.
Suppose the population of ants in my yard is given by
\begin{equation*} A =f(t)= 100(1.12)^t \end{equation*}
where \(A\) represents the number of ants, and \(t\) is time in weeks since I started measuring.
  1. Is the number of ants increasing or decreasing? By what percentage is it increasing/decreasing each week?
  2. When will the population of ants reach 1000?
Solution
  1. It is increasing because the growth factor \(1.12\) is greater than 1. It increases by 12% per week

  2. According to the graph, during week 20 the ant population reaches 1000.
