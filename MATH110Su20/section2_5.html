Section 2.5 Preview of Composite and Inverse Functions

Worksheet 2.5.1 Worksheet

1.
If \(f(x) = \sqrt{x+4}\) and \(g(x)=x^2\text{,}\) compute
  1. \(\displaystyle f(g(1))\)
  2. \(\displaystyle g(f(1))\)
  3. \(\displaystyle f(g(x))\)
Solution
  1. \(\displaystyle f(g(1))=f(1^2)=f(1)=\sqrt{1+4}=\sqrt{5}\)
  2. \(\displaystyle g(f(1))=g(\sqrt{1+4})=g(\sqrt{5})=(\sqrt{5})^2=5\)
  3. \(\displaystyle f(g(x))=f(x^2)=\sqrt{x^2+4}\)
2.
Let \(C=f(w)\) be the cost in dollars producing \(w\) widgets in our widget factory. What do the quantities \(f(500)\) and \(f^{-1}(20000)\) represent in terms of widgets and dollars? Solution
The quantity \(f(500)\) represents the cost of producing 500 widgets. The quantity \(f^{-1}(20000)\) represents the number of widgets that can be produced for $20000.
3.
Below is a graph of \(f(x).\)

  1. If \(g(x)=x^2+3\text{,}\) compute \(g(f(1))\text{.}\)
  2. Compute \(f(f(-4))\text{.}\)
Solution

  1. Since the \(y\) coordinate of the point with \(x=1\) is 0, \(g(f(1))=g(0)=0^2+3=3\text{.}\)
  2. Note that \(f(-4)=0\) as the function crosses the \(x\) axis there. Hence, \(f(f(-4))=f(0)=-0.5\text{.}\)
