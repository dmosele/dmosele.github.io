The key to this one is taking the natural logarithm of both sides (a preview of the next section).

\begin{equation*} \ln(e^{0.12x}) = \ln(100) \quad \Rightarrow \quad 0.12x = \ln(100) \end{equation*}

We get the second equality by asking "what power of \(e\) is it?"

\begin{equation*} x = \frac{\ln(100)}{.12} \approx 38.376 \end{equation*}
