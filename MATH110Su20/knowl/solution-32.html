Solution 5.1.1.1.1.
  1. \(\log(10000) = 4\) because \(10^4=10000\)
  2. \(\ln(e^3)=3\) If we ask, what power of \(e\) it is, we see the answer is 3.

  3. \(\log\left( \frac{1}{\sqrt{10}} \right)=-\frac 12\) There are several ways to do this, but notice that we could look at it as

    \begin{equation*} \log(1)-log(\sqrt{10})=0-\frac 12 = -\frac 12 \end{equation*}
  4. \(e^{\ln 2}=2\) by our fifth log property

  5. \(\log(\sqrt{1000})=\frac 32\) There are several ways to approach this, but using the fact that square root is the same as the \(\frac 12 \) power,

    \begin{equation*} \log(\sqrt{1000}) = \log(1000^{\frac 12}) = \frac 12 \log(1000) = \frac 12 \cdot 3 = \frac 32 \end{equation*}
